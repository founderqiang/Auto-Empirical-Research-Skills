# Edmans (2024) Rejection Guardrails — Quick Reference

Source: Alex Edmans, "Learnings From 1000 Rejections," Financial Management, 2025, 54:419-444.
Based on 1,059 manuscripts handled as editor/managing editor of Review of Finance (2016-2022).

## Category 1: CONTRIBUTION Problems

### 1.1 Insufficiently Novel (Convex Combination)
- If X→Z and Z→Y are known, showing X→Y adds little
- Extending US results to another country without institutional reasons for different predictions
- Extending from one type of investment to another (capex → R&D)
- Extending peer effects from one corporate policy to another
- TEST: "Would a reader Bayesian update much after reading this paper?"

### 1.2 Insufficiently Important
- "Just another determinant of Y" added to a long list
- "Just another outcome of X" added to a long list
- A "curiosum" — interesting but not first-order
- Documenting channels that don't change interpretation of results
- TEST: "Would a survey paper on Y mention your X?"
- TEST: "Would a policymaker change behavior based on this finding?"

### 1.3 Doesn't Fit General-Interest Finance
- Topic is really accounting, OB, or macro
- Topic is too niche for general audience
- Bibliography check: if most references are to field journals, submit to a field journal

### 1.4 Insufficiently Generalizable
- Single-event study trying to draw general conclusions
- Narrow sample with broad claims

### 1.5 One Side of Trade-off
- Studying whether X creates value but only documenting benefits
- Must address both costs and benefits

### 1.6 Lacks Clear Hypotheses
- No directional predictions
- "Kitchen-sink" regressions without theory
- Hypothesis about Y1 but studying Y2 (complements vs substitutes unclear)
- "It's an empirical question" is a weak defense

## Category 2: EXECUTION Problems

### 2.1 Instrumental Variables
- Must explain IV in introduction, not bury it
- Must justify BOTH relevance AND exclusion restriction
- "Finding valid instruments is very difficult" — burden is on the author

### 2.2 Measurement Issues
- Proxy doesn't precisely measure the phenomenon
- Quantity vs quality confusion (e.g., counting votes ≠ governance quality)

### 2.3 Robustness
- Results must survive alternative measures, controls, specifications
- Small samples may be driven by outliers

### 2.4 Alternative Explanations
- Beyond reverse causality and omitted variables
- Even causal results can have multiple interpretations
- Showing X causes Y doesn't mean X is good/bad without interpretation

## Category 3: EXPOSITION Problems

### 3.1 Overclaiming
- Claiming causation from correlation
- Claiming generalizability from narrow sample
- Title/abstract promising more than paper delivers

### 3.2 Insufficient Motivation
- Not explaining WHY the question matters
- Not connecting to broader economic significance

## The Golden Rules for Proposal Design

1. **Change the reader's prior** — if a reader spends hours on your paper, they should learn something they didn't know or couldn't have guessed
2. **Pass the survey paper test** — would this finding get its own paragraph in a future survey?
3. **Every hypothesis needs a direction** — no fishing expeditions
4. **Identification must be front and center** — explain your strategy in the introduction
5. **Acknowledge limitations honestly** — editors respect transparency
6. **Mechanisms matter when they change interpretation** — not all channels are equally interesting
