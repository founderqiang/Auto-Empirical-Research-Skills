# Style Rules Checklist — from McCloskey, *Economical Writing* + Strunk & White Ch.V

Use this checklist during Pass 2 (Style and Readability) of the polishing workflow.

## McCloskey's Core Rules (from *Economical Writing*, 3rd ed.)

### Clarity is the Only Golden Rule (Ch.4)
- "Write not merely so that the reader can understand, but so that he cannot possibly misunderstand" — Quintilian
- If a conscientious reader finds a passage unclear, it has to be rewritten — Karl Popper
- Clarity is a social matter; the reader, not the writer, decides what is clear

### Writing Is Thinking (Ch.2)
- Content and style cannot be separated — they are "yolk and white in a scrambled egg"
- You discover what you really think by writing it down — the writing itself is an act of thinking
- "The line of words is a miner's pick, a woodcarver's gouge, a surgeon's probe" — Annie Dillard

### Avoid Boilerplate (Ch.12)
- **Never start with "This paper..."** — use a hook instead
- **Never use the table-of-contents paragraph**: "The outline of this paper is as follows..."
- **Never anticipate**: "As we shall see" — useless and irritating
- **Never summarize excessively**: "I am going to say; I say it; I have said it" — the Jonesian Arrangement — is boring
- **Avoid "background"** = padding = material you collected but later discovered was beside the point
- **Avoid the academic pose** — theoretical chatter copied out of a textbook. Apply it, don't repeat it.
- **Avoid boilerplate in theoretical framing**: "Consider two cities, A and B, trading an asset, X" → "New York and London in 1870 both had markets for Union Pacific bonds."

### A Paragraph Should Have a Point (Ch.14)
- Every paragraph must tell the reader something new
- **Graff's trick**: End each paragraph with a simple, street-talk sentence that Mom could understand instantly
- Paragraphs should not be too long (exhausting) or too short (breathless)

### Control Your Tone (Ch.13)
- Choose and maintain an appropriate implied author (persona)
- Avoid invective — anger undercuts your standing: "The furious behavior of an angry man is more likely to exasperate us against himself than against his enemies" — Adam Smith
- Delete or tone down adverbs that drive emotional pressure: *absolutely, purely, very*
- Keep opinions to yourself unless relevant — "To air one's views gratuitously is to imply that the demand for them is brisk" — Strunk & White
- Use humor if you can — "Only third-rate scholars insist on their dignity"
- Reveal more of your character — the worst mistake is to be unpleasant

### Use Verbs, Active Ones (Ch.25)
- **Write with nouns and verbs, not with adjectives and adverbs** — in revision, adjectives and adverbs should be the first to go
- **Use active voice**: "You should use active verbs" not "Active verbs should be used"
- **Find the action and express it in a verb** (avoid nominalization):
  - "There is a data reanalysis need" → "We must reanalyze our data"
  - "The left has widespread disdain for profit-driven healthcare" → "The left disdains profit-driven healthcare"
  - "Self-interested exchange is a knowledge-generating process" → "Self-interested exchange generates knowledge"
- Circle every *is* in a draft; if the page looks like acne, replace with real action verbs
- "There is" and "It is" often signal nominalization

### Avoid Elegant Variation (Ch.20)
- **One word = one meaning**: Use the same word for the same concept throughout
- Do NOT vary words just for elegance: don't call it "industrialization", then "growing structural differentiation", then "economic development"
- "The new economic history is concerned not only with what happened but also with why events turned out as they did" → wrong: "what happened" and "events that turned out as they did" refer to the same thing
- BUT avoid pointless repetition when the meanings differ

### Be Concrete (Ch.27)
- Prefer the specific to the general: "Au Bon Pain baguette" → "bread" → "widgets" → "X"
- Singulars are more concrete than plurals
- Readers find it harder to translate abstractions down into examples than the reverse
- "Had the machines and men of 1860 embodied the same knowledge of how to spin cotton as in 1780..." not "...the same technology..."

### Be Plain (Ch.28)
- Prefer Anglo-Saxon to Latinate words: *need* over *necessitate*, *buy* over *purchase*, *feel* over *experience a sensation of*
- "Geographical and cultural factors function to spatially confine growth to specific regions for long periods of time" → "It's a good bet that once a place gets poor it will stay poor"
- Untie "teutonisms" (piled-up nouns): "factor price equalization" → "the equalization of the prices of factors"
- Reexamine any phrase with more than one adjective — lean it out
- Watch for nouns used as adjectives in chains: "contractually uniform transaction cost"

### Avoid Words That Bad Writers Love (Ch.26)
**Bad Words to flag and replace:**

Vague nouns: *concept, data* (misused), *function* (for *role*), *situation, individuals* (for *people*), *agents* (same), *structure, process* (usually empty — "the transition process" → "the transition"), *time frame* (=time), *the turn of the XIXth century*

Pretentious verbs: *critique* (for *criticize*), *implement, comprise, analyze* (overused for *discuss*), *hypothesize* (for *suppose*), *finalize, state* (for *say*), *try and* → *try to*

Pointless adjectives: *former...latter* (make reader look back), *aforementioned, intra/inter* (use *within* and *between*), *interesting* (weak), *kind of/sort of/type of* (vague)

Useless adverbs: *fortunately/interestingly* (cheap opinion insertions), *respectively* (avoid entirely), *very* (most things aren't very), *for convenience* (all writing should be)

Clumsy conjunctions: *due to* (use *because*), *in terms of*, *plus* for *and*, *not only...but also* (callow Latinism — the best writers never use it)

### Avoid This, That, These, Those (Ch.30)
- Circle every *this, these, those* in the draft — you'll be shocked
- Replace with plain *the* or *it* or *such* in most cases
- *This* points the reader backward, interrupting forward flow

### Avoid Cheap Typographical Tricks (Ch.29)
- Use scare quotes sparingly — if you use "quotation marks" for "sarcasm" or to "apologize" for a word, don't
- Avoid excessive italics — if emphasis isn't natural from sentence structure, fix the sentence
- Limit acronyms: "An occasional GDP won't hurt, but GDCF pains even the hardened"
- Know your tribe's conventions: ragged right for academia, full-justify for business

### The Order Around Switch Until It Good Sounds (Ch.23)
- English word order is flexible — try different arrangements until the sentence works
- **The end is the place of emphasis** — put the most important word/phrase last
- The beginning is second in emphasis
- Line up series by increasing length: "Keynes and the Keynesians" works; "The Keynesians and Keynes" doesn't
- Put modifiers close to what they modify

### Use Your Ear (Ch.18) / Read Out Loud (Ch.24)
- Read everything out loud in revision — you'll hear problems you can't see
- Don't write anything you'd be embarrassed to read aloud to the intended audience
- Vary sentence length and structure (Gardner's rule: elaborate only ONE of subject/verb/object)
- Read good writing (poetry, the best old books) to train your ear

### Tables, Graphs, Equations Must Be Self-Explanatory (Ch.15)
- The reader should understand the point without referring to the main text
- Use declarative sentence titles: "All Conferences Should Happen in the Midwest" not "A Model of Transport Costs"
- Use meaningful, spelled-out names, not alphanumeric codes
- Write equations with words, not just symbols: "Quantity of Grain = 3.56 + 5.6(Price of Grain) − 3.8(Real Income)"
- Avoid "chart junk" (pointless ornamentation) — Edward Tufte's rule

### Speak to an Audience (Ch.11)
- Choose a reader and stick with her — a real person (your colleague, your advisor)
- Ask: "How would [this specific reader] see this?"
- Keep the argument at one consistent level of difficulty
- Read your draft immediately after reading excellent prose — the good rhythm will carry over for ~30 minutes

## Strunk & White Ch.V — An Approach to Style (21 Reminders)

### 1. Place yourself in the background
Write to draw attention to the substance, not to the author's mood and temper.

### 2. Write in a way that comes naturally
Use words and phrases that come readily — but don't assume natural = flawless.

### 3. Work from a suitable design
Anticipate the shape of what is to come.

### 4. Write with nouns and verbs
"The adjective hasn't been built that can pull a weak or inaccurate noun out of a tight place."

### 5. Revise and rewrite
Revising is part of writing. Few writers produce what they're after on the first try.

### 6. Do not overwrite
Rich, ornate prose is hard to digest. Ruthlessly delete the excess.

### 7. Do not overstate
A single overstatement diminishes the whole.

### 8. Avoid the use of qualifiers
*Rather, very, little, pretty* — "the leeches that infest the pond of prose, sucking the blood of words."

### 9. Do not affect a breezy manner
The breezy style is often the work of an egocentric.

### 10. Use orthodox spelling
*Nite, thru, pleez* are not acceptable.

### 11. Do not explain too much
Be sparing with adverbs after "he said" / "she replied."

### 12. Do not construct awkward adverbs
Don't write *tangledly* or *tiredly*. Words not used orally are seldom the ones to put on paper.

### 13. Make sure the reader knows who is speaking
In dialogue, place attributives where the break would come naturally.

### 14. Avoid fancy words
"Avoid the elaborate, the pretentious, the coy, and the cute." Anglo-Saxon > Latin. *Gut* is lustier than *intestine*, but both have their place.

### 15. Do not use dialect unless your ear is good

### 16. Be clear
"Be obscure clearly! Be wild of tongue in a way we can understand!" When hopelessly mired, start fresh — don't fight through terrible syntax.

### 17. Do not inject opinion
"To air one's views gratuitously is to imply that the demand for them is brisk."

### 18. Use figures of speech sparingly
Similes in rapid fire are distracting. Don't mix metaphors.

### 19. Do not take shortcuts at the cost of clarity
Write things out. "The longest way round is usually the shortest way home."

### 20. Avoid foreign languages
Write in English.

### 21. Prefer the standard to the offbeat
"Finalize is not standard; it is special, and peculiarly fuzzy and silly." Err on the side of conservatism, on the side of established usage.
