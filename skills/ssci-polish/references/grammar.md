# Grammar Rules Checklist — from Strunk & White, *The Elements of Style* (4th ed.)

Use this checklist during Pass 1 (Grammar and Mechanics) of the polishing workflow.

## I. Elementary Rules of Usage

### 1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's
- Apply regardless of the final consonant: *Charles's friend*, *Burns's poems*
- Exceptions: ancient proper names in *-es* and *-is* (Jesus', Moses'); *for conscience' sake*
- Pronominal possessives (*hers, its, theirs, yours, ours*) have no apostrophe
- **Common error**: *it's* (contraction of "it is") vs. *its* (possessive)

### 2. In a series of three or more terms, use a comma after each term except the last
- The Oxford/serial comma: *red, white, and blue*
- In business firm names, follow the firm's own usage

### 3. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas
- *The best way to see a country, unless you are pressed for time, is to travel on foot.*
- Never omit one comma and leave the other
- Nonrestrictive relative clauses are parenthetic and need commas
- Restrictive clauses are NOT set off by commas: *People who live in glass houses shouldn't throw stones.*
- Names/titles in direct address are parenthetic
- Abbreviations *etc., i.e., e.g.* and academic degrees are parenthetic
- **No comma** before a restrictive term of identification: *Billy the Kid*, *The novelist Jane Austen*
- When a main clause is preceded by a phrase or subordinate clause, use a comma to set off these elements (but see McCloskey's counterpoint: commas are often unnecessary before short introductory phrases, especially before *the*)

### 4. Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause
- *The early records of the city have disappeared, and the story of its first years can no longer be reconstructed.*
- Two-part sentences with *as* (=because), *for*, *or*, *nor*, *while* (=and at the same time) also need the comma
- If a dependent clause precedes the second independent clause, no comma after the conjunction
- When the subject is the same and expressed only once: comma if connective is *but*; omit if connective is *and* and relation is close

### 5. Do not join independent clauses with a comma
- Use a semicolon: *Mary Shelley's works are entertaining; they are full of engaging ideas.*
- Or write as two sentences
- Exception: a comma is acceptable when clauses are very short and alike in form (*Man proposes, God disposes.*)
- When the second clause is preceded by an adverb (*accordingly, besides, then, therefore, thus*), the semicolon is still required

### 6. Do not break sentences in two
- Do not use periods for commas: *I met them on a Cunard liner many years ago. Coming home from Liverpool to New York.* (Wrong → replace period with comma)
- Permissible only for deliberate emphasis: *Again and again he called out. No reply.*

### 7. Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list, appositive, amplification, or illustrative quotation
- A colon tells the reader that what follows is closely related to the preceding clause
- Do NOT separate a verb from its complement or a preposition from its object with a colon
- Join two independent clauses with a colon if the second interprets or amplifies the first
- Functions: formal letter salutation, time notation, title/subtitle separation, Bible chapter/verse

### 8. Use a dash to set off an abrupt break or interruption and to announce a long appositive or summary
- A dash is stronger than a comma, less formal than a colon, more relaxed than parentheses
- Use only when a more common mark seems inadequate

### 9. The number of the subject determines the number of the verb
- Words between subject and verb do not affect the number
- In *one of those who...*, the relative clause takes a plural verb
- Singular verb after *each, either, everyone, everybody, neither, nobody, someone*
- *None*: singular when meaning "no one" or "not one"; plural when suggesting more than one
- Compound subjects joined by *and* take plural verb; but inseparable compounds take singular (*Bread and butter was all she served.*)
- Singular subject remains singular even with *with, as well as, in addition to, except, together with, no less than*
- A linking verb agrees with the number of its subject

### 10. Use the proper case of pronoun
- *Will Jane or he be hired, do you think?*
- *The culprit, it turned out, was he.*
- The case of *who* depends on its function in its clause
- A pronoun in a comparison is nominative if it is the subject of a stated or understood verb: *Sandy writes better than I. (Than I write.)*
- Use the simple personal pronoun as subject (not *myself, yourself*)
- Gerunds usually require the possessive case: *Mother objected to our driving.*
- Present participles as verbals take the objective case: *They heard him singing.*

### 11. A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject
- *Walking slowly down the road, he saw a woman...* (correct: *he* is walking)
- *Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house very cheap.* (wrong: *I* am not dilapidated)
- Same rule applies to phrases preceded by conjunctions/prepositions, nouns in apposition, adjectives

## II. Words and Expressions Commonly Misused

Check for these common errors:
- **Aggravate** = to add to a troublesome condition; **Irritate** = to annoy
- **All right** — always two words
- **Allude** (indirect mention) vs. **Elude** (evade); not synonymous with *refer*
- **Alternate** (every other one) vs. **Alternative** (one of two possibilities)
- **Among** (more than two) vs. **Between** (two, or more than two considered individually)
- **And/or** — damages sentences; avoid
- **Anticipate** vs. **Expect** — use *expect* for simple expectation
- **As good or better than** — should be *as good as, or better than*
- **As to whether** — *whether* is sufficient
- **Being** — not appropriate after *regard...as*
- **But** — unnecessary after *doubt* and *help*
- **Can** = able; not a substitute for *may*
- **Case** — often unnecessary (*In many cases* → *Many*)
- **Certainly** — overused intensifier
- **Character** — often redundant (*acts of a hostile character* → *hostile acts*)
- **Claim** — means "lay claim to"; not a substitute for *declare, maintain, charge*
- **Compare to** (point out resemblances between different orders) vs. **Compare with** (point out differences between same order)
- **Comprise** = embrace, include (the whole comprises the parts, not vice versa)
- **Consider** — not followed by *as* when meaning "believe to be"
- **Contact** — vague; prefer *get in touch with, phone, meet*
- **Data** — plural; *these data are...*
- **Different than** → *different from*
- **Disinterested** = impartial; **Uninterested** = not interested
- **Due to** → *because of, through, owing to* (correct only when = attributable to)
- **Effect** (noun: result; verb: bring about) vs. **Affect** (verb: influence)
- **Enormity** = monstrous wickedness, not bigness
- **Etc.** — avoid in formal writing; not to be used after *such as, for example*
- **Fact** — use only for matters of direct verification
- **Factor** — hackneyed; usually replaceable
- **Farther** (distance) vs. **Further** (time, quantity)
- **Finalize** — pompous, avoid (prefer *finish, complete*)
- **Fortuitous** = happening by chance; not *fortunate* or *lucky*
- **Hopefully** — means "with hope," not "I hope"
- **However** — avoid starting sentences with *however* meaning "nevertheless"
- **Imply** (suggest) vs. **Infer** (deduce)
- **Importantly** — avoid; *More importantly* → *What's more* or *More important*
- **In regard to** — not *in regards to*
- **Interesting** — unconvincing introduction word; make it interesting, don't label it so
- **Irregardless** → *regardless*
- **-ize** — don't coin verbs with this suffix; prefer existing verbs
- **Lay** (transitive) vs. **Lie** (intransitive)
- **Leave** vs. **Let** — *Let it stand*, not *Leave it stand*
- **Less** (quantity) vs. **Fewer** (number)
- **Like** (governs nouns/pronouns) vs. **As** (governs phrases/clauses)
- **Loan** (noun) vs. **Lend** (verb)
- **Most** — not for *almost* in formal writing
- **Nauseous** (sickening) vs. **Nauseated** (sick at stomach)
- **Nice** — imprecise; use sparingly; best for "precise" or "delicate"
- **Nor** — often wrongly used for *or* after negative expressions
- **One** — in the sense of "a person," followed by *one's*, not *his* or *her*
- **Partially** (to a degree, of conditions) vs. **Partly** (a part distinct from whole)
- **Presently** — ambiguous; best restricted to "in a short while"
- **Shall** (first person future) vs. **Will** (second/third person future; first person determination)
- **So** — avoid as an intensifier in writing
- **Split infinitive** — avoid unless special emphasis on the adverb is needed
- **That** (defining/restrictive) vs. **Which** (non-defining/nonrestrictive)
- **The fact that** — debilitating expression; revise out of every sentence
- **They** — do not use with singular antecedents (*each, everyone, everybody*)
- **This** — the pronoun *this* referring to complete sense of preceding clause often produces imprecise statement
- **Unique** = without like or equal; no degrees of uniqueness
- **Utilize** → *use*
- **Verbal** (word for word) vs. **Oral** (spoken)
- **Very** — use sparingly
- **While** — avoid indiscriminate use for *and, but, although*; use only for "during the time that"
- **-wise** — not to be used indiscriminately as a pseudosuffix
- **Would** — for habitual action; when habit is already expressed (*once a year, every day*), past tense without *would* is sufficient

## III. Punctuation Conventions

- Two spaces after a period (in manuscripts; journals will adjust)
- One space after comma, semicolon, colon
- In US publications: close quote goes OUTSIDE the comma/period (*"thus."*)
- In author-date citations: *"a quotation" (Author 2020).* — quote mark → citation in parentheses → period
- Oxford/serial comma: REQUIRED before *and* in series
- Avoid the comma splice — do not join two independent clauses with just a comma
- Use commas by ear, not by rigid rule — especially don't always comma after introductory phrases
